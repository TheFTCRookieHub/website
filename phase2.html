Phase 2: The Mid-Season Grind

Weeks 5-8: Iteration, Reliability, and Survival

The "Messy Middle"

Included in this phase is the reality check. Your first robot version probably works, but not well enough. Things might be breaking. This phase is about Iterative Design‚Äîthe cycle of testing, failing, and improving.

üí° Golden Rule: Fail Fast

Don't spend 3 weeks building the "perfect" mechanism only to find out it doesn't fit on the field. Build quick prototypes with cardboard or wood first. If it works in cardboard, it will work in metal.

Unbreakable Reliability

A reliable robot that scores 20 points every match beats a robot that scores 100 points once and dies twice.

Notebooking the Chaos

Judges love seeing the failure. If your intake arm snapped in half, take a picture of it before fixing it.

What to log daily:

  • The Problem: "Intake kept jamming on game piece."
  • The Idea: "Try adding a compliant wheel."
  • The Test: "Tested with 5 game pieces. 4 worked, 1 jammed."
  • The Result: "Better, but needs more speed. Will try higher gear ratio."

Case Study: The Claw That Failed

Scenario: Team 9999 built a beautiful 3D printed claw. It worked perfectly in the lab. At the first competition, it shattered in Match 2.

Why it failed:

  • Printed with 10% infill (too weak).
  • Bolts were tightened too much, cracking the plastic.
  • No spare parts were packed.

The Fix (Iterative Loop):

  1. Redesign: Thicker walls, 50% infill.
  2. Test: Dropped it on concrete 5 times. It survived.
  3. Document: Wrote a "Failure Report" in the notebook (Judges LOVED this).

Wiring Standards Deep Dive

Bad wiring kills more robots than bad code. Follow these rules or die.

1. Wire Gauge (AWG)

Part Min Gauge
Battery 14 AWG (Thick)
Motors 18 AWG
Servos 22 AWG (Thin)

2. Connector Management

  • XT30: Yellow connectors for power. Never use Tamiya (white/cheap).
  • JST PH: The tiny 4-pin sensor cables. Treat them gently.
  • Logic Level Converter: Required if using 5V sensors on 3.3V hub.
